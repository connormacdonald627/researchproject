Introduction

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are widely used protocols for communication between devices in IoT (Internet of Things) networks. However, the performance and overhead of these protocols remain obscure in different IoT network scenarios.

IoT in this situation refers to the edge networking layer of systems, in which devices such as sensors and other embedded devices are connected to a centeral node, such as a gateway or server, to transmit or recieve data. Assessing the performance of these protocols in this context cruical due to the unique characteristics of IoT networks, such as limited throughput, high latency, and an increased number of connected devices. I will assess the performance of UDP/TCP protocols in this context under wireless IoT network conditions.

These protocols behave very differently: TCP prioritises reliability and is connection-oriented, retransmitting packets when acknowledgements from the destination are not received. UDP, on the other hand, is connectionless and does not guarantee delivery, sending packets without confirmation. [1]

The performance of these protocols varies depending on a multitude of factors, including but not limited to packet size, network topology and throughput. To investigate these variables, NS2 (Network Simulator 2) and NAM (Network Animator) will be utilized to assess performance metrics such as packet loss by varying bandiwdth and node count in an IoT networking scenario. The variance of upstream and downstream traffic flow will also be examined to review the performance of these protocols under a diverse array of network conditions.

Data Analysis

In this simulation scenario, I have 6 nodes sending upstream to a central gateway node and send packets of size 512 bytes. The rate of packet transmission is varied from 50,000 bps to 1,000,000 bps with it approximately doubling each step.

Bandwidth & Throughput

Through experimentation and NS2 simulation results, it was observed that TCP signficiantly underperformed UDP in terms of throughput, with TCP achieving a maximum rate of 2,091 Kbps whilst UDP achieved a maximum rate of 4,187 Kbps which is approximately double the output of TCP; This is likely due the implemention of TCP's reliability mechanisms, such as slow start and congestion control, along with overhead from acknowledgements and retransmissions of dropped data packets.

Packet Loss

Whilst TCP's reliabbility mechanisms signficantly reduced throughput, it also resulted in a critical reduction in packet loss. With TCP achieving a maximum packet loss rate of 5% compared to UDP's maximum packet loss rate of 33%. Its worth noting that TCP at 500,000 bps had a packet loss rate of 0.39% which may be due to TCP's ability to adapt to network conditions using its slow start mechanism which allows it to gradually increase its transmission rate until it finds an optiomal rate for sending data without overwheling the network; leading to a significant reduction in packet loss at higher throughputs.
