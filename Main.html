Introduction

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are widely used protocols for communication between devices in IoT (Internet of Things) networks. However, the performance and overhead of these protocols remain obscure in different IoT network scenarios.

IoT in this situation refers to the edge networking layer of systems, in which devices such as sensors and other embedded devices are connected to a central node, such as a gateway or server, to transmit or receive data. Assessing the performance of these protocols in this context cruical due to the unique characteristics of IoT networks, such as limited throughput, high latency, and an increased number of connected devices. I will assess the performance of UDP/TCP protocols in this context under wireless IoT network conditions.

These protocols behave very differently: TCP prioritises reliability and is connection-oriented, retransmitting packets when acknowledgements from the destination are not received. UDP, on the other hand, is connectionless and does not guarantee delivery, sending packets without confirmation. [1]

The performance of these protocols varies depending on a multitude of factors, including but not limited to packet size, network topology and throughput. To investigate these variables, NS2 (Network Simulator 2) will be utilized to assess performance metrics such as packet loss by varying bandwidth and node count in an IoT networking scenario. The variance of upstream and downstream traffic flow will also be examined to review the performance of these protocols under a diverse array of network conditions.

Data Analysis

Bandwidth & Throughput

In this simulation scenario, I have 6 nodes sending upstream to a central gateway node and send packets of size 512 bytes. The rate of packet transmission is varied from 50,000 byte/s to 1,000,000 byte/s with it approximately doubling each step. Each experiment runs for 20 seconds and the performance of TCP and UDP is assessed by measuring the total throughput and packet loss from the raw data from each NS2 simulation.

Through experimentation and NS2 simulation results, it was observed that TCP significantly underperformed UDP in terms of throughput, with TCP achieving a maximum rate of 2,091 Kbps whilst UDP achieved a maximum rate of 4,187 Kbps which is approximately double the output of TCP; This is likely due to the implementation of TCP's reliability mechanisms, such as slow start and congestion control, along with overhead from acknowledgements and retransmissions of dropped data packets; The consistency of the variance in TCP and UDP's throughput across the range of bandwidths indicates that TCP's reliability mechanisms impose a fixed overhead of approximately 50% of the bandwidth capacity, regardless of the total network load.

Packet Loss

Whilst TCP's reliability mechanisms significantly reduced throughput, it also resulted in a critical reduction in packet loss. With TCP achieving a maximum packet loss rate of 5% compared to UDP's maximum packet loss rate of 33%. It's worth noting that TCP at 500,000 byte/s had a packet loss rate of 0.39% which may be due to TCP's ability to adapt to network conditions using its slow start mechanism which allows it to gradually increase its transmission rate until it finds an optimal rate for sending data without overwhelming the network; leading to a significant reduction in packet loss at higher throughput.

Node Count

In simulation scenarios with varying numbers of nodes, TCP and UDP's throughput and packet loss both have a positive correlation with node count. As the number of nodes increases, the throughput of both protocols increases due to more node data being transmitted to the central gateway, along with an increase in packet loss due to network congestion.

Packet Loss Compromises

Figure B shows that TCP's reliability mechanisms, such as acknowledgements and retransmissions, significantly reduce packet loss compared to UDP, which lacks these features. As node count increases packet loss across all protocols increase due to network congestion due to the central gateway becoming a bottleneck for the whole network as all nodes send packets simultaneously to a single gateway. Implications for IoT Network Design and Topology

The results from the simulation scenarios with varying node counts indicate that there is a compromise between throughput and reliability as node count increases, which aligns with my findings from the bandwidth simulation scenarios.

In high density IoT sensor networks; Networks that prioritise throughput may need to accept higher packet loss rates and may be better suited to protocols such as UDP, while networks that prioritise data integrity and reliability may need to accept lower throughput and may be better suited to protocols such as TCP.

Regardless of protocol choice, the central gateway always becomes a bottleneck as node count increases. Multiple gateways or a different network topology, such as a hierarchical structure may be better adapted to such scenarios to reduce the bottleneck in high density IoT networks and improve overall performance as the network scales in real world deployments.
